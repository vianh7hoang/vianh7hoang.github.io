Which birth control method is right for me?

My Choice For All is developed to help assigned female at birth persons determine which birth control methods are potentially best for them. This tool creates a customized recommendation based on user past experiences, personal and medical preferences, and medical history.

This tool is for informational purposes only and does not constitute as medical advice, diagnosis, or treatment. If you have a medical problem or concern, please contact your health care provider.


What is Birth Control?

Birth control, or contraception, refers to medication or medical devices intended to prevent pregnancy and comes in many methods, including condoms, hormonal birth control pills, or long-acting reversible contraceptives. While mainly used for pregnancy prevention, birth control can be used to manage menstrual cycles, lessen the symptoms of medical conditions, and prevent sexually transmitted infections (STIs).


Types of Birth Control

There are several forms of birth control. Barrier methods, such as condoms or internal condoms, protect users from STIs. There are also hormonal birth control options, like the combination pill, the hormonal intrauterine device, and the ring, that include hormones like progestin, estrogen, or a combination of the two. Progestogen is a synthetic version of the hormone progesterone, which mainly functions to prepare the endometrium, the lining of the uterus, for fertalization. If there is no pregnancy, the endometrium sheds, and if there is a pregnancy, progesterone increases support for the pregnancy. Estrogen is a steroid hormone which mainly functions to support the development of secondary sex characteristics (breasts, hips, etc.), menstruation, pregnancy, and menopause. Each method functions on a different schedule as some methods are used daily or for each count of sexual intercourse and some only need to be replaced weekly, monthly, or yearly. Some forms of birth control require your sexual partner to participate in the birth control method, such as condoms, spermicide/vaginal gel, fertility awareness methods (FAM), and withdrawal.


Transgender Assigned Female at Birth (AFAB) Persons and Birth Control

There is misinformation and a lack of education regarding the use of contraception by transgender assigned female at birth (AFAB) persons, people whose sex does not align with their gender identity. Transgender individual or gender non-conforming persons may sometimes suffer from gender dysphoria. Gender dysphoria presents itself in many ways, such as top gender dysphoria (regarding the chest region), bottom gender dysphoria (regarding menstruation, menstruation symptoms, or vaginal insertion), and gender dysphoria regarding pregnancy or delivery.


Many transgender AFAB persons believe that going through gender-affirming testosterone hormone replacement therapy (HRT) is enough to prevent pregnancy, but these persons are still at risk of unintended pregnancy. In a 2021 study of transgender persons experiencing pregnancy, 54% of the pregnant population had unintended pregnancies. Furthermore, transgender AFAB persons may not use contraception due to the obstacles this population face in accessing quality health care, as 30% of transgender persons have delayed or avoided seeking necessary health care secondary to discrimination and 15% of transgender persons have been refused health care due to their gender identity.


The goal of MyChoiceForAll is to provide educational information and resources for transgender AFAB person regarding their reproductive health care and birth control decision making process. MyChoiceForAll will take in input from users and recommend birth control methods based on user preferences and experiences, taking into account any present forms of gender dysphoria, as well as connect users to transgender-friendly providers and resources.

Condoms Condoms are thin rubber sheaths that are worn one's penis during intercourse. They are the most commonly used method to prevent pregnancy and sexually transmitted infections (STIs).

Internal Condoms Internal condoms are thin rubber sleeves that are inserted into one's vagina during intercourse. They are commonly used to prevent pregnancy and sexually transmitted infections (STIs).

Cervical Cap Cervical caps are a small silicone cap that is inserted into one's vagina up to six hours prior to intercourse. They are the typically paired with spermacide or vaginal gel.

Fertility Awareness Method Fertility awareness methods (FAM) are ways to track your ovulation to prevent pregnancy. There are three different methods commonly used - the Temperature Method, the Cervical Mucus Method, and the Calendar Method. A combination of these three methods is the most effective at preventing pregnancy.

Arm Implant The arm implant is a tiny, thin rod that is placed on the inside of your upper arm beneath the skin. It releases the hormone progestin into your body to prevent you from getting pregnant and it is effective for up to 5 years.

Copper IUD An intrauterine device (IUD) is a small piece of flexible plastic that is placed inside the uterus to prevent pregnancy. The copper IUD (known as the Paragard IUD) is wrapped in copper, which repels sperm to prevent pregnancy. It can prevent pregnancy for up to 12 years.

Hormonal IUD An intrauterine device (IUD) is a small piece of flexible plastic that is placed inside the uterus to prevent pregnancy. The hormonal IUD uses the hormone progestin to prevent pregnancy. It can prevent pregnancy between 3 to 5 years, depending on the brand used.

The Patch The birth control patch is a small patch that is placed on certain areas on your body and releases both the hormones estrogen and progestin into your body through your skin. When used correctly, it is highly efficient at preventing pregnancy and has other health benefits as well.

Combo Pills The combination (combo) birth control pill is a type of medicine that contains both the hormones estrogen and progestin. You pick up the pills monthly from a pharmacy or clinic and take one pill every day. The pill is very effective at preventing pregnancy when taken consistently at the same time.

Mini Pills The mini birth control pill is a type of medicine that contains only the hormone progestin. You pick up the pills monthly from a pharmacy or clinic and take one pill every day. The pill is very effective at preventing pregnancy when taken consistently at the same time.

The Ring The birth control ring is a small, flexible ring that is worn inside of your vagina and releases both estrogen and progestin into your body. When used correctly, it is highly efficient at preventing pregnancy and has other health benefits as well.

The Shot The birth control shot is an injection that is administered by a health care professional every three months. It contains the hormone progestin to prevent pregnancy. When used on a regular schedule, it is very effective at preventing pregnancy.

Sterilization Sterilization refers to tubal ligation, hysterectomy, or endometrial ablation. Tubal ligation is a surgical procedure to tie the fallopian tubes. Hysterectomy is surgical procedure to remove the ovaries and uterus. Endometrial ablation is surgical procedure to destroy the lining of uterus. Although all three are costly, they are extremely effective at preventing pregnancy.

Spermicide/ Vaginal Gel Spermicide or vaginal gel refers to a chemical gel that is placed in the vagina prior to intercourse meant prevent pregnancy.

Withdrawal Withdrawal refers to removal of the penis from the vagina prior to ejaculation.

The Patch

What is it?

The birth control patch is a small patch that is placed on certain areas on your body and releases both the hormones estrogen and progestin into your body through your skin. When used correctly, it is highly efficient at preventing pregnancy and has other health benefits as well.

How does this fit me?

  • It is cost effective with many insurance plans

  • It is patch is maintained on a weekly schedule.
  • It does require your intimate partner to participate in the birth control process.
  • They do not require undergoing a medical procedure.
  • You have indicated past positive interactions with it.

This method does not directly match your preferences, but is commonly used by women your age.

How do I use this?

The patch requires a prescription from your health care provider. Each prescription has 3 patches and lasts for 1 months. Each week, open a pouch and place the patch on clean, dry skin on your belly, upper outer arm, buttocks, or back. At the end of the week, peel off the patch and apply a new one. Every fourth week, you have the option to take a week break to allow for your period to start, or apply a new patch to skip your period altogether.

Sterilization

What is it?

Sterilization refers to tubal ligation, hysterectomy, or endometrial ablation. Tubal ligation is a surgical procedure to tie the fallopian tubes. Hysterectomy is surgical procedure to remove the ovaries and uterus. Endometrial ablation is surgical procedure to destroy the lining of uterus. Although all three are costly, they are extremely effective at preventing pregnancy.

How does this fit me?

  • It is more than 99% effective at preventing pregnancy.
  • It can help manage cramps and other side effects associated with periods.
  • It can help manage conditions such as PCOS or endometriosis.
  • It uses zero hormones.
  • It is permanent and lasts a lifetime.
  • It does require your intimate partner to participate in the birth control process.

This method does not directly match your preferences, but is commonly used by women your age.

How do I use this?

To receive sterilization, you must make an appointment with your health provider. After a routine check up and evaluation by a provider, you will undergo surgery that can require a recovery period of approximately 2-3 weeks. During the procedure, you will be placed under anesthesia so you will not feel or remember anything during the procedure.
